John is building a forensic identity profile of himself — not a personality assessment, not a self-help exercise, but a cold, evidence-based reconstruction of who Joao Mercado actually is. The model is archaeological: multiple dig sites (conversations, behavioural data, external observer data), cross-referenced for convergence, with contradictions treated as signal rather than noise.

The name "WHOAMI" is deliberate — this is a system command, not a question.

## PART 2: FORENSIC DATA COLLECTION PROTOCOL

### Overview

Three parallel data streams, each capturing different evidence types. The picture emerges from convergence AND contradiction between streams.

```
STREAM A: Conversation Extractions (narrator-present)
STREAM B: Behavioural Data Mining (narrator-absent)  
STREAM C: External Observer Data (narrator-displaced)
         ↓              ↓                ↓
    ┌────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │     WHOAMI PROFILE (convergence +      │
    │     contradictions = identity)          │
    └────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### STREAM A: Conversation Extractions

**Source:** Past and future Claude conversations, text messages, voice memos, any context where John is narrating or thinking aloud.

**Method:**
1. Copy full conversation text into a file
2. Submit to Claude with extraction prompt (template below)
3. Each extraction produces a numbered data point file
4. Cross-reference across extractions for recurring patterns and contradictions

**Extraction Prompt Template:**
```
You are a forensic identity analyst. The attached file contains a conversation. 
Your task: extract every data point that reveals something about the identity, 
cognitive patterns, values, fears, capabilities, blind spots, and behavioural 
tendencies of the person named John (Joao Mercado).

Classify every data point as:
- STATED: directly declared by the subject
- DEMONSTRATED: observed in real-time behaviour during conversation
- INFERRED: reasonable deduction from multiple data points
- STRUCTURAL: recurring architecture/pattern across domains

Pay special attention to:
- What the subject does vs. what they say they do
- Moments of self-correction, redirection, or flinching
- Failed metaphors (metaphors reached for but that don't quite fit)
- What's conspicuously absent
- How the subject handles being wrong or uncertain

Do NOT flatter the subject. Do NOT resolve contradictions. 
Flag them as open evidence.

Output format: numbered data points in a table with columns for 
[#, Data Point, Evidence Type, Confidence (High/Medium/Low), Notes]
```

**Quality control:** Every 5 extractions, run a meta-analysis looking for:
- Data points that recur across 3+ sources (high confidence structural)
- Data points that appear once and contradict the majority pattern (investigate, don't discard)
- Themes that are suspiciously coherent (possible narrator artefact)

### STREAM B: Behavioural Data Mining

**Source:** Digital records that contain no narrative — raw behavioural traces.

#### B1: Bank / Financial Records
| What to Extract | What It Reveals |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Spending categories over time | Actual values vs. stated values |
| Impulse vs. planned purchases | Decision-making patterns, dopamine-driven spending |
| Time-of-day spending patterns | Circadian regulation, medicated vs. unmedicated behaviour |
| Subscriptions (active and forgotten) | Abandoned intentions — projects started and silently dropped |
| Spending on medication pause days vs. normal days | Behavioural change without narrator filter |
| Ratio of tool/hardware purchases to other categories | Tests the "tools-as-possibility" hypothesis with hard data |

**Method:** Export bank statements as CSV. Submit to Claude with:
```
Analyse these financial records for behavioural patterns. The subject is 
not present — extract what the spending reveals about decision-making 
style, impulse patterns, actual priorities (vs. stated ones), and any 
temporal patterns (time of day, day of week, monthly cycles). 
Flag anomalies and absences.
```

#### B2: SMS / Messaging Records
| What to Extract | What It Reveals |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Who John initiates contact with vs. who contacts him | Relationship hierarchy — who he seeks vs. who seeks him |
| Response latency by contact | Who matters enough for immediate response |
| Tone shifts between recipients | Which relationships get the narrator, which get the raw version |
| Topics John raises vs. topics raised with him | What he's thinking about vs. what others associate him with |
| Messages drafted but not sent (if accessible) | Suppressed impulses — the narrator's edit history |
| Frequency patterns over time | Relationship investment trajectories |

**Method:** Export message history. Submit to Claude for pattern analysis, redacting sensitive content from third parties as needed.

#### B3: Digital Behaviour Traces

### STREAM C: External Observer Data

**Purpose:** Capture how others perceive John, specifically in ways that differ from his self-perception.

#### C1: The Interview Protocol

**Who to interview:** 3-5 people from different life contexts. Ideal mix:
- A family member (mother Ines is the obvious candidate — she has the longest observational dataset)
- A friend from the trades/work context
- A friend from the intellectual/AI context (Seon)
- The supervisor who said "learn to be happy with your lot" (if relationship permits)
- Someone who has known John less than 2 years (captures current presentation, not historical narrative)

**Who conducts the interview:** NOT John. Options:
1. A freelance qualitative researcher (university postgrad, $30-50/hr) — gold standard
2. A trusted friend who doesn't know the interviewees — workable
3. A structured written questionnaire sent by John with a framing letter — lowest quality but still valuable

**Framing for interviewees:**
"John is working on a personal development project and has asked me to interview people in his life as part of it. There are no right or wrong answers. He specifically asked for honesty over kindness — he wants to understand how people actually experience him, including the parts that are frustrating or confusing."

#### C2: The Interview Questions

**SECTION 1: Concrete Incidents (bypasses social filtering)**
1. Tell me about a specific time John surprised you.
2. Tell me about a specific time John frustrated you or let you down.
3. Tell me about a time you saw John completely absorbed in something. What was it? What did he look like?
4. Tell me about a time John said or did something you didn't understand at the time but that made sense later.
5. Tell me about a time you and John disagreed about something. How did he handle it?

**SECTION 2: Perception Gaps (captures how others read him)**
6. If you had to explain John to someone who'd never met him, what would you say? (Note: first thing they say is the dominant perception)
7. What do you think John is best at? What would he say he's best at? Are those the same thing?
8. What do you think John wants most? (Do NOT prompt with options)
9. What do you think gets in John's way most?
10. Is there something about John that you think he doesn't see about himself?

**SECTION 3: Relationship Dynamics (captures relational patterns)**
11. When John needs help, how does he ask? (Or does he?)
12. When you need something from John, how does he respond?
13. Has your perception of John changed over time? How?
14. What's the thing about John you'd most want to tell him but haven't?

**SECTION 4: The Silence Question (Errol Morris technique)**
15. Is there anything else? (Then wait. Do not fill the silence. The thing they say after the pause is often the most honest.)

#### C3: Photo / Object Elicitation (Optional, High Value)

Show the interviewee a photo from a shared context (a worksite, gathering, John's garage) and ask:
- "What was happening here?"
- "Where was John in this?"
- "What was he doing / saying?"

The narrative reveals positioning and perception without direct evaluation.

#### C4: Triangulation Method

For any significant incident or period, attempt to get accounts from at least 2-3 people. **Do not harmonise conflicting accounts.** The contradictions are primary evidence. Document:
- What each person emphasises
- What each person omits
- Where John appears in their version of events
- How their account differs from John's known self-narrative

### STREAM D: Self-Administered Tools (Supplementary)

#### D1: Modified Life Story Interview (McAdams Protocol)

Record yourself (audio, not written — writing engages the narrator more) answering:
1. Divide your life into chapters. Give each a title. (Structure reveals what the narrator treats as significant)
2. Describe a high point — a specific moment, not a period.
3. Describe a low point — same specificity.
4. Describe a turning point — a moment after which things were different.
5. Your earliest memory.
6. A scene involving shame or embarrassment.
7. A scene involving a moral decision.
8. What is your personal myth — the story you tell yourself about who you are?

**Critical:** Also ask these same 8 questions to 2-3 interviewees (about John, not about themselves). The delta between John's self-narrative and others' narratives is where occluded material lives.

#### D2: The Flinch Log

Keep a running note (phone, quick capture) every time you notice:
- A thought you started and redirected
- A topic you avoided or moved past quickly
- A moment of discomfort you smoothed over
- Something someone said that landed wrong but you didn't address
- A compliment that felt inaccurate but you accepted

These are real-time captures of the narrator editing in progress. Don't analyse them — just log them. Analysis comes later in batch.

#### D3: The Contradiction Log

Whenever you notice yourself doing something that contradicts the emerging WHOAMI profile, log it. Examples:
- "The profile says I'm driven by tools-as-possibility but today I walked past Bunnings and felt nothing"
- "The profile says I don't seek reassurance but I just fished for a compliment from [person]"
- "The profile says I think in systems but this problem I just approached purely emotionally"

These are the most valuable data points in the entire project.

## PART 3: DATA MANAGEMENT

### File Naming Convention
```
WHOAMI-A-[source]-[date].md     → Conversation extractions
WHOAMI-B-[type]-[date].md       → Behavioural data analysis  
WHOAMI-C-[interviewee]-[date].md → External observer data
WHOAMI-D-[tool]-[date].md       → Self-administered tools
WHOAMI-META-[date].md           → Cross-stream meta-analysis
```

### Cross-Referencing Schedule
- After every 3 new data files: run pattern check for recurring data points
- After every 5 new data files: run contradiction audit
- After every 10 new data files: produce updated WHOAMI profile draft
- The profile draft is always provisional — it's a working hypothesis, not a conclusion

### Evidence Confidence Levels
| Level | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **CONFIRMED** | Appears in 3+ independent sources across 2+ streams |
| **PROBABLE** | Appears in 2+ sources OR 1 source with strong corroboration |
| **PROVISIONAL** | Single source, internally consistent, awaiting cross-validation |
| **CONTESTED** | Contradicted by at least one other data point — flag for investigation |
| **OCCLUDED** | Suspected but only visible through absence, flinch, or narrator avoidance |

## PART 4: WHAT "DONE" LOOKS LIKE

The WHOAMI profile is never finished — identity isn't static. But a **working draft** is ready when:

1. At least 10 conversation extractions completed (Stream A)
2. At least 1 behavioural data set analysed (Stream B)
3. At least 3 external interviews completed (Stream C)
4. At least 1 self-administered tool completed (Stream D)
5. At least 2 cross-stream meta-analyses performed
6. The profile contains at least 5 CONTESTED or OCCLUDED data points (if it doesn't, the methodology isn't working)

The profile should read like a dossier, not a résumé. It includes strengths, deficits, blind spots, contradictions, and open questions. It is written in third person. It does not flatter or pathologise. It describes a system.
